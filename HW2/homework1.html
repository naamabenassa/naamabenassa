As an afterthought, I will summarize some of the implications of what I have written about intersubjectivity, especially in relation to the discussion developed over the last decade. I use the term intersubjectivity not to refer to the generalization in which we act in the presence of two people - in a purely interpersonal field, but rather to the particular matter of recognizing one another as an equal center of being. My use is derived from a philosophical process carried out by Habermas out of the superiority of a subject who knows or transforms the object into a subject that communicates with other subjects.




This line of thinking, which returns to Hagal, is somewhat different from that of Stolorow and Atwood (1992; Atwood and Stolorow, 1984), their definition of intersubjectivity refers to the interplay between different subjective worlds. This definition does not tell us how the intersubjective differs from the interpersonal, a term that used to denote the matrix of relationships that make up the life of the soul. Nor does it sufficiently differentiate between subjects and objects, such as the supreme issue of how a child comes to know his mother as a separate subject and does not refer to her as an object. In my opinion, only by speculating on such recognition can we give interestivity a clear and specific meaning.
